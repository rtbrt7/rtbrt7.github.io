Research Summary

I am a prospective PhD candidate in Economics, with wide-ranging interests in Trade and Labour Economics, with special interest in the spatial analysis of these topics.

In my MPhil thesis, I have examined how globalisation could affect workforce composition (regular workers and contract workers) of industries and thereby quality of jobs, a key labour market indicator. I have studied this question by taking into account how globalisation has altered the market structure of the formal Indian manufacturing industries and how it has affected the workforce composition. The study required the combination of firm level panel data and factory level panel data, collected from two different sources.

In an inflexible labour market like India, my findings provide significant evidence of Indian firms responding to globalisation by hiring relatively more of contract workers. Our finding identifies an important indirect way in which globalisation could affect labour market conditions and worker welfare.

How Dodd-Frank Act and Capital Market Incentives Deter Large Banks from Lending to Small Businesses

Xuan Zou
Job Market Paper

Abstract

This paper aims to investigate the magnitude and evolution of Small Business Lending by the U.S. banks and financial incentives on banksâ small business lending (SBL) before and after the financial crisis. The main finding is that although the capital market overall encourages U.S. banks to lend to small businesses, the incentives to banks of different sizes are different and have changed since the financial crisis. Specifically, banks with more than $10 billion consolidated assets were encouraged to lend to Small Businesses before the financial crisis, but the capital market has been penalizing mid-tier and large banks for SBL since 2007. For community banks, the financial incentives for SBL has been consistently positive, even during the financial crisis.

Can the Greater Fool Theory Explain Stock Market Bubbles? Evidence from China

Xuan Zou
Under Review

Abstract

Many have noticed the phenomenon that naive investors are attracted to the market as stock prices soar, yet few empirical studies have tested for this bubble phenomenon. This paper presents previously unused data on the aggregate number of newly opened brokerage accounts in China and tests the role of new investors in bubble formation. I find that new investors, attracted by soaring stock prices and the intensive trading activities of others, drove the Chinese stock market bubbles in 2007 and 2015, supporting the Greater Fool theory of bubbles. The inexperienced and naive new investors appear more likely to be the "greater fools." Using the residual orthogonalization method, I build a data-driven structural model system, where shocks from the new accounts variable explain 40-55% of Chinese stock return variation.

The Construction of Bank Holding Company-Level Data -- Using the Call Reports, Y-9C Reports, Summary of Deposits, and Other Data

Xuan Zou
Data Manual

Abstract

The purpose of this manual is to provide detailed information about how to construct bank holding company-level data using raw regulatory data from the Federal Reserve System and other sources. The data construction process is very complex due to many reasons. First, the definitions of bank accounting variables are complicated and changing over time, so they should be defined and clarified carefully. Second, different types of banks report to Federal Reserve System, the Federal Deposit and Insurance Corporation (FDIC), the comptroller of the Currency, and the Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council (FFIEC) with responsibilities of Community Reinvestment Act (CRA), so the sample should be filtered according to certain criteria. Third, the bank-level data needs to be aggregated to bank holding company-level data, because the banks under the same bank holding company will behave collectively. Lastly, some control variables, such as GDP and the Herfindahl-Hirschman index (HHI), need to be weighted by the bank deposit share in each state.

This manual combines bank holding company accounting data collected from Federal Reserve Y-9C reports , market value data from Wharton Research Data Service (WRDS) Compustat, Small Business Lending (SBL) data from the Consolidated Reports of Condition and Income (the Call Reports) , branch-level bank deposit data from Summary of Deposit , and state-level GDP data from Bureau of Economic Analysis . The time period of interest is 2001-2016, covering both before and after the financial crisis. Except for SBL data, other data is collected at the end of each year during 2001-2016. The methodology of this manual basically follows Hughes, Jagtiani, and Mester (2016) and Hughes and Moon (2018).
